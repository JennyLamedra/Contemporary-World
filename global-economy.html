The Global Economy

Robert Sugden

International Monetary Fund (IMF) – An international organization with 183 member countries that promotes monetary cooperation, exchange stability, economic growth, and provides short-term financial assistance to countries facing balance of payments adjustments (IMF, 2019).

Economic Globalization – The expansion of national economies into a global market driven by modern technologies and institutional setups that promote faster and easier flow of goods and capital (Sugden, 2005).

Global Economy – Refers to the interconnectedness of various countries' economies from extraction, production, distribution, consumption, to disposal of goods and services (Schilirò, 2018).

Daniele Schilirò

International Financial Institutions (IFIs) – Global financial institutions that support a country's economic growth through loans, technical assistance, and other support to governments and private sectors.

Defining Economic Globalization

Economic globalization relates to the expanding scale of international trade in goods and services. It is propelled by global economic integration, linking disparate production processes into cohesive products and services. Although it has historical origins in ancient trade and empire-building, modern economic globalization is characterized by a neoliberal approach that reduces government roles and favors private sector influence. Today, developed nations lead to the restructuring of global economies.

Actors That Facilitate Economic Globalization

  • International Economic Organizations (e.g., IMF, World Bank, OECD) – Develop trade discussions, promote neoliberal policies, and facilitate regional agreements like ASEAN and NAFTA.
  • Multinational Corporations (MNCs) – Their numbers rose from 44,000 in 1996 to 88,000 in 2006; they are pivotal to economic globalization.
  • Central Banks – Exert significant influence over national economies, often surpassing politicians in economic matters.
  • Global Civil Society – Advocates for alternative perspectives and policies, especially during global events like the 1992 UN Conference on Environment and Development.
  • Transnational Advocacy Networks (TAN) – Organize to promote causes and ideals, challenging negative impacts of economic globalization.
  • G8 and G20 – Advisory groups of nations that discuss economic and political problems and influence national legislative regulations.

Immanuel Wallerstein

The Modern World System (Wallerstein's World-System Theory)

Immanuel Wallerstein's world-system theory emphasizes the economic world system over traditional nation-state-based analyses. A world system is a social system with boundaries, structures, member groups, rules of legitimation, and coherence (Wallerstein, 2011).

The global economy is divided into three tiers:

  • Core states – Centers of major demand for goods and services; advanced production and innovation occur here.
  • Peripheral areas – Sources of raw materials and basic production; often exploited by core regions.
  • Semi-peripheries – Intermediate zones that process and distribute products between peripheries and cores.

THE GLOBAL INTERSTATE SYSTEM

Global Interstate System – An institutional arrangement of governance that addresses regional or globalized issues beyond the scope of a nation-state (Chase-Dunn, 1981).

Internationalism – Emphasizes diversity, multiculturalism, and political, economic, and cultural cooperation between nations.

Globalism – An ideology advocating free flow of people, goods, and information across borders, with a stronger focus on economic exchanges.

Christopher Chase-Dunn

Effects of Globalization on Governments

As globalization intertwines nations through politics, trade, and communication, the role of governments evolves. National policies now reflect both local and international realities. For example, the Montreal Protocol successfully addresses global environmental issues through international cooperation.

However, globalization also challenges local governments, especially in attracting foreign investment. Local initiatives to establish manufacturing firms may displace farmers and convert agricultural land into industrial zones, raising concerns about job creation versus social and environmental impacts. Local governments struggle to balance economic growth with community welfare under corporate influence.

Institutions That Govern Globalization

• Non-State Actors – NGOs and networks gain influence through community appealing strategies, especially on issues like climate change.

• Glocalization – Authority shifts to actors above or below the state; increasing participation of new actors as nation-state roles declines.

• United Nations (UN) – With 192 members, it facilitates global governance.

• Regional Commissions (ECA, ECE, ECLAC, ESCAP, ESCWA) – Debate and create laws for economic and social development.

• World Bank Group – Founded in 1944 at Bretton Woods; provides loans for projects in developing countries through IBRD, IDA, IFC, and MIGA.

Globalism vs. Internationalism

  • Internationalism – Involves political, economic, and cultural cooperation between nations; emphasizes diversity and multiculturalism (Roudometof, 2005; Sargent, 2008).
  • Globalism – Ideology favors free flow of people, goods, and information across borders; focuses more on economic exchanges (Roudometof, 2005; Sargent, 2008).
  • Similarities – Both describe increasing global interconnectivity through communications, transport, and technology; often used interchangeably.

Victor Roudometof

Assumptions Behind the Global Interstate System

The global interstate system assumes that competing states cannot fully control each other, so a governance framework is needed to address globalization. International cooperation through institutions like the UN and World Bank is seen as necessary to manage issues like trade, environment, and security.

Corporations
Corporations are private entities producing goods/services. TNCs give foreign branches some independence, while MNCs keep control centralized in their home country.

Transnational Corporations (TNCs) are multi-country companies that use foreign labor and resources to drive global production and investment while being centrally managed from their home country.

Multinational Corporations (MNCs) are companies that operate globally but keep core operations centralized in their home country, mainly for export.

Market Integration

Market Integration involves the interconnection of economies through:

  • Global Sourcing - Products are sourced, made, and distributed across different countries.
  • Philippines' BPO - The PH holds 18% of the global BPO market with 851 companies generating US$24.4B.
  • Integration Types - Horizontal (acquiring competitors) and Vertical (owning supply chain stages).
  • Key Drivers - IFIs and global corporations push market integration forward.

International Financial Institutions: Role in Global Economy

IFIs differ from private institutions by prioritizing development over profit, offering financial aid and expert guidance. Major IFIs like the IMF and World Bank support countries through loans, infrastructure funding, and policy reforms while working alongside the private sector.

Contributions to development

Are essential to global progress, focusing on social and economic development through financial and technical support rather than profit.

Key Issues with IFIs

Legitimacy: Critics question fairness due to dominance by rich nations in shareholders, policymaking, and leadership (e.g., Europeans leading IMF, Americans the World Bank), advocating for merit-based selections over national origins.

Effectiveness: Development programs are scrutinized for controversies like large-scale projects displacing indigenous peoples, requiring social safeguards for human rights and the environment, with the Paris Declaration and Common Performance Assessment System improving monitoring.

Conditionality: Loans often require conditions such as privatization, trade liberalization, subsidy elimination, and limits on public investments, which critics argue impose inappropriate Western free-market policies on developing countries.

Financial Capacity and Sustainability: IFIs face reduced income amid rising demand for global initiatives; middle-income countries avoid loans due to high costs and conditions. Shifts from loans to grants strain sustainability, though the 2002 Monterrey Conference pledged increased support from developed nations

Global Governance

  • Global governance refers to how the international system provides cooperation, rules, and public services without a single world government.
  • It involves states, international organizations, NGOs, corporations, and individuals working together to solve global problems.
  • Global governance studies how the world is governed and how governance systems change over time.
  • Modern global issues such as climate change, terrorism, cyber threats, pandemics, and financial instability challenge existing political structures.
  • There is a growing gap between rapidly evolving global problems and outdated international decision-making systems.

Key Elements of the Nation-State (Hobsbawm, 1996)

The nation-state, according to Eric Hobsbawm, is defined by a unified and sovereign territory governed by a central authority that holds legal and coercive power. It directly rules over citizens without intermediaries, administers governance centrally, and gains legitimacy from the people it represents. Ideally, the population shares a common national identity and unity.

The Rise of Non-State Actors

The Rise of Non-State Actors

The rise of non-state actors emerged after the Cold War as global governance became more decentralized and multipolar. International organizations, private groups, and transnational businesses increasingly participate in solving global issues through partnerships across sectors. Compared to traditional state bureaucracies, these flexible networks allow faster and more efficient action while expanding diversity in global decision-making and cooperation.

The United Nations

United Nations is a major international organization that promotes global peace, security, human rights, economic development, and social progress through international cooperation. It operates through five main branches to help enforce international law and address global issues.

  • UN General Assembly: Main decision-making body made up of all member states; promotes UN principles through policies and recommendations under an elected president.
  • UN Security Council: Responsible for maintaining international peace and security by authorizing military actions, ceasefires, and sanctions; composed of 5 permanent and 10 rotating members.
  • International Court of Justice: Resolves legal disputes between countries and provides legal advice to UN organizations.
  • Economic and Social Council (ECOSOC): Supports economic and social development and strengthens cooperation among member nations.
  • Secretariat: Led by the Secretary-General; provides research, reports, and administrative support for UN operations.

The G20+ and a New Framework for Global Cooperation

The G20+ is proposed as a coordinated and accountable body linked to the United Nations to help maintain global economic stability and strengthen international cooperation. Its main goal is to promote inclusive economic growth that benefits all nations and people fairly and equitably.

Core Functions

  • Facilitate multi-stakeholder, cross-disciplinary dialogue and policy solutions.
  • Promote inclusive economic reform.
  • Enable global economic crisis response.

Structure, Membership and UN ties

The G20+ includes details on its composition and connections to the UN, ensuring representation and accountability within the broader global economic cooperation framework.
