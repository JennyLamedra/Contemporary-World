Legal Basis of International Relations

The Legal Basis of International Relations refers to the laws, principles, treaties, and customs that govern the relationships among states and other international actors. It establishes rules that promote peace, cooperation, justice, and stability in the international community. International law regulates diplomacy, trade, territorial disputes, human rights, and other global concerns.

National Law

National law refers to laws that regulate individuals, organizations, and institutions within a state. These laws are enforced by the government of a particular country.

International Law

International law consists of rules and principles that regulate the conduct and relations of states and international persons. It governs how countries interact with one another in matters involving diplomacy, war, treaties, trade, and human rights.

Treaty

A treaty is a written international agreement between states governed by international law. Treaties may be contained in one document or several related documents and are legally binding once ratified.

INTERNATIONAL LAW AS PART OF PHILIPPINE LAW

International Law as Part of Philippine Law

The 1987 Philippine Constitution recognizes international law as part of the country's legal system through the Doctrine of Incorporation and the Doctrine of Transformation.

Philippine Constitutional Framework for Treaties

Under the 1987 Philippine Constitution, treaties ratified by the President require concurrence by at least two-thirds of all Senate members before they become valid and effective.

Doctrine of Incorporation

This doctrine states that generally accepted principles of international law automatically become part of Philippine law even without additional legislation.

Doctrine of Transformation

This doctrine requires international agreements, especially treaties, to undergo domestic legislative processes before becoming enforceable within the Philippines. Treaties negotiated by the President require concurrence by at least two-thirds of the Senate.

INTERNATIONAL CONVENTIONS

International Conventions

International agreements between states, in written form, governed by international law. They can be in a single instrument or multiple related ones (Art. 2(1)(a), Vienna Convention on Law of Treaties).

Bilateral Conventions

Bilateral conventions are agreements involving two states only. Examples include defense treaties and extradition agreements.

Multilateral Conventions

Multilateral conventions are agreements involving several states. Examples include UNCLOS, ICCPR, CEDAW, and CRC.

International Customary Law

International customary law consists of rules derived from consistent state conduct based on the belief that such conduct is legally required.

CUSTOMARY LAW

Essential Elements:

  • State Practice: Special or local custom refers to long-standing practices between states that create mutual rights and obligations.
  • Opinio Juris: Opinio juris refers to the belief that a practice is legally obligatory under international law.

Obligations Erga Omnes

Obligations Erga Omnes are obligations owed to the international community as a whole because they protect shared international values and human dignity.

Jus Cogens

Jus Cogens, also known as compelling law, refers to peremptory norms with supreme authority in international law. These norms override treaties and ordinary customary rules and cannot be modified except by another rule of the same nature.

TREATIES AND INTERNATIONAL AGREEMENTS

Steps in Treaty Making

  • Negotiation: States discuss and draft terms.
  • Signing: Representatives formally sign the agreement.
  • Exchange of Ratification Instruments: Formal exchange to confirm intent.
  • Ratification: Constitutional approval is completed.
  • Registration and Publication: By the UN Secretariat for transparency and record keeping.

A treaty is a binding international agreement made between states and governed by international law. The treaty-making process involves negotiation, signing, exchange of ratification instruments, ratification, registration, and publication.

Obligations Pending Entry into Force

States that sign a treaty must avoid actions that would defeat the treaty's object and purpose before it officially enters into force.

LEGAL BASIS OF INTERNATIONAL LAW

General Principles of Law

These are fundamental legal principles recognized across major domestic legal systems globally (such as good faith, res judicata, and equity) that help fill gaps where no specific treaty or custom applies.

Judicial Decisions

Rulings by international bodies like the International Court of Justice (ICJ) serve as subsidiary means for determining rules of law. While they don't create binding precedent for all, they highly influence international jurisprudence.

Teachings of Highly Qualified Publicists

The writings, analyses, and publications of the world's foremost legal scholars and international law experts serve as an additional subsidiary source to clarify, interpret, and guide the application of international legal norms.

STATES AND SOVEREIGNTY

State

A state is an entity recognized under international law possessing a permanent population, defined territory, government, and sovereignty.

Elements of a State

The essential elements of a state include permanent population, defined territory, government, and sovereignty.

Creation of States

States may be created through discovery and occupation, prescription, cession, accretion, or conquest.

State Recognition

State recognition refers to the acknowledgment by one state of another state's existence and its capacity to engage in international relations.

Rights of States

States possess rights such as independence, equality, self-defense, territorial integrity, jurisdiction, and diplomatic relations.

NATIONALITY, REFUGEES, AND STATELESSNESS

Nationality

Nationality refers to the legal relationship between an individual and a state based on genuine connection, shared interests, and reciprocal rights and duties.

Refugees

A refugee is a person outside their country who cannot return because of fear of persecution due to race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or social group membership.

Non-Refoulement

  • Non-refoulement is the principle that refugees must not be returned to places where they may face danger or persecution.

Asylum

  • Asylum refers to the temporary protection granted by another state to refugees and displaced persons.

Jus Soli

  • Jus soli is the acquisition of nationality based on place of birth.

Jus Sanguinis

  • Jus sanguinis is the acquisition of nationality based on blood relationship.

Naturalization

  • Naturalization is the legal process through which a foreigner acquires nationality.

STATELESSNESS AND LAW OF THE SEA

Statelessness

Statelessness refers to the condition of a person who is not recognized as a national by any state under its laws.

Causes of Statelessness

Statelessness may result from loss of nationality, gaps in nationality laws, discrimination, or state succession.

Law of the Sea

The Law of the Sea is a body of international rules governing maritime affairs and the use of oceans.

Baselines

Baselines are reference lines used to measure maritime zones.

Territorial Sea

  • The territorial sea extends up to 12 nautical miles from the baseline and is under the sovereignty of the coastal state.

Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ)

  • The Exclusive Economic Zone extends up to 200 nautical miles where a state has sovereign rights over natural resources.

High Seas

  • The high seas are areas beyond national jurisdiction that are open to all states and not subject to sovereignty claims.

Continental Shelf

  • The continental shelf refers to the seabed and subsoil extending beyond the territorial sea where states may explore and exploit resources.

HUMAN RIGHTS AND OCEAN GOVERNANCE

International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea (ITLOS)

ITLOS was established under UNCLOS and settles disputes related to maritime law and ocean governance.

Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)

The UDHR, adopted by the United Nations in 1948, declares that all people are born free and equal in dignity and rights.

Civil and Political Rights

Civil and political rights include the rights to life, liberty, equality before the law, freedom of religion, freedom of expression, and protection against torture and slavery.

Economic, Social, and Cultural Rights

Economic, social, and cultural rights include the rights to work, education, social security, adequate living standards, and participation in cultural life.

International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR)

The ICCPR protects rights such as self-determination, freedom from torture, freedom from slavery, and freedom of thought and religion.

STATE RESPONSIBILITY & HUMAN RIGHTS LAW

State Responsibility

State responsibility arises when a state commits an internationally wrongful act against another state, establishing an obligation to make reparations.

Internationally Wrongful Act

  • An act or omission that constitutes a breach of an international obligation binding upon the state.

Attribution

  • The conduct in question must be legally attributable to the state under international law, usually through the actions of its organs or officials.

Reparation

  • The responsible state is under an obligation to make full reparation for the injury caused, whether material or moral.

International Human Rights Law

International Human Rights Law lays down the obligations of governments to act in certain ways or refrain from certain acts to promote and protect fundamental human freedoms.

Treaty-Based Bodies

  • Committees of independent experts established to monitor the implementation of core international human rights treaties by state parties.

Charter-Based Bodies

  • Institutions established directly under the United Nations Charter, such as the UN Human Rights Council, which hold broad mandates to address human rights issues globally.
