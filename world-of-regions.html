The Global Divides: The North & The South

Since the process of globalization is uneven, it follows that there is an imbalance in the socio-economic and political categories of the world. The world is divided into north and south, and first, second, and third.

GLOBAL SOUTH

The term Global South is a metaphor for interstate inequality and a product of Western imagination (Claudio, 2014). Historically, there had been divisions and labeling among the different nations of the world.

Reflection of Global divide Between North & South

This reflects the global divide between the Global North and Global South as experienced by Overseas Filipino Workers (OFWs). Many OFWs in Europe, Australia, and America observe significant differences in living conditions between the Philippines and developed Western countries.

While they often earn higher incomes abroad, some return home briefly as "one-day millionaires," sharing gifts with family, relatives, and communities. Despite their financial contributions through remittances and taxes, they continue to recognize the economic challenges faced in the Philippines. Ultimately, their visits remain temporary, as they must return overseas to sustain their livelihoods and continue working abroad.

Latin America refers to countries in the southern part of the American continent that were colonized by Spain. In 1494, the Treaty of Tordesillas divided newly discovered lands outside Europe between Spain (West) and Portugal (East), shaping early colonial territories.

From these historical divisions, the concept of the Global South emerged, referring to countries primarily in Africa, Latin America, and Asia, including the Middle East. These nations are generally characterized by socio-economic and political differences compared to countries in the Global North, which are often associated with stronger economies, fair labor practices, free trade, lower tariffs, and sustainable development policies.

The Global South also connotes developing countries as opposed to rich, industrialized, and wealthy nations

WHAT IS GLOBAL NORTH?

The Global North refers to developed and industrialized countries, including members of the Group of Eight (G8): Canada, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, the United Kingdom, Russia, and the United States. It also includes the permanent members of the United Nations Security Council and other highly developed nations such as Australia and New Zealand, as well as advanced economies in Asia. According to Claudio (2014), the Global South is continuously constructed and redefined by dominant global powers, while also being reshaped through resistance and movements from below.

The concept of the Global South is not limited to developing countries, as similar conditions such as poverty, inequality, unfair labor practices, and human rights issues can also be found in developed nations like Europe, Australia, the United States, and Canada. This shows that experiences associated with the Global South are present across different parts of the world. Conversely, individuals from rural or less developed areas, such as Tupi in South Cotabato, may experience places like Metro Manila as resembling the Global North due to its advanced infrastructure, financial systems, commercial establishments, and role as the center of government and economic activity.

GLOBAL NORTH

The terms Global South and Third World are closely related and often used to describe conditions in developing countries, although "Third World" is considered the earlier concept that was later replaced by "Global South" after the Cold War (Claudio, 2014). Historically, countries were classified based on political and economic systems during the Cold War, where the world was divided between Western capitalist states and Soviet socialist states. Capitalism promotes consumer choice, private property, and economic freedom, while socialism is based on state control of production, distribution, and exchange. Under this classification, capitalist countries were labeled as First World, while socialist countries were categorized differently within the global system.

REGIONALISM VS REGIONALIZATION

Regionalism

  • Refers to the deliberate political process where states within a region create formal agreements, organizations, or institutions to cooperate and address shared challenges.
  • It includes policies designed to strengthen economic, political, or security cooperation.
  • Examples include regional organizations like ASEAN.

Regionalization

  • Refers to the natural or market-driven process where countries in a geographic area become more interconnected through trade, investment, migration, culture, and communication.
  • It happens as globalization encourages stronger economic and social links among neighboring states.
  • Often develops without formal political agreements.

Key Difference:

Regionalization = spontaneous integration through economic and social interaction.

Regionalism = planned cooperation through political and institutional efforts.

REGIONAL ALLIANCES & NON-STATE ACTORS

Official Intergovernmental Alliances

Formal regional organizations are established by sovereign states to build geopolitical presence. Key global examples include the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN), the European Union (EU), and the African Union (AU).

Through mutual agreements, member nations synchronize trade regulatory standards, facilitate cleaner border migrations, and establish shared security protocols to protect regional stability.

Non-State Regionalism From Below

Regionalism is not solely dictated by presidents and prime ministers. Non-State Regionalism refers to bottom-up regional formatting led by civil society networks, non-governmental organizations (NGOs), and grassroots initiatives.

These transnational organizations collaborate across borders to advocate for issues that states might minimize, such as environmental preservation, indigenous protections, labor rights, and democratic transformations.

Contemporary Friction Points

Despite the advantages of integration, regional blocs routinely face intense internal and external structural challenges:

Sovereignty vs. Integration: Member states frequently clash when centralized regional policies conflict with domestic laws and local national security agendas.

Economic Asymmetry: Wealth disparities between highly industrialized member states and developing nations within the same bloc can lead to unfair resource distribution and labor exploitation.

THE GLOBAL DIVIDE & REGIONALISM

The Global Divide

The distinction between the Global North and Global South is heavily anchored in socio-economic and political stratification rather than strict geography. The Global North represents wealthy, technologically advanced, and highly industrialized nations, whereas the Global South encompasses developing economies navigating systemic resource challenges and historical dependencies.

The Dynamics of Regionalism

Regionalism serves as a crucial mechanism for states to navigate this global divide. By establishing official regional institutions, proximate countries pool their economic markets and political leverage. These alliances allow smaller or developing regions to stand resilient against the aggressive market pressures of dominant global superpowers.

Conclusion: Ultimately, regional alignment is not simply about proximity; it is a calculated geopolitical strategy. Whether through state-led policies (Regionalism) or market-driven integrations (Regionalization), forming regions allows nations to collectively address economic vulnerabilities, security issues, and cultural protections on the global stage.

SOURCES OF INTERNATIONAL LAW

1. Treaties and Conventions

Treaties and conventions represent the most direct, formal, and explicit source of international law, consisting of written agreements between two or more nations that create legally binding obligations on the parties involved. These formal instruments can be bilateral (between two states) or multilateral (involving multiple states), and they cover virtually every aspect of international relations including trade, human rights, environmental protection, arms control, and diplomatic relations.

Treaties are known by various names including conventions, protocols, covenants, charters, statutes, and agreements, but all share the common characteristic of being governed by the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (1969), which establishes the rules for treaty formation, interpretation, and termination. The process of creating a treaty typically involves negotiation, signature, ratification, and entry into force, with each stage requiring specific legal procedures and state consent.

Examples of major treaties include the United Nations Charter, the Geneva Conventions on humanitarian law, the Paris Agreement on climate change, and numerous bilateral trade agreements. Treaties can establish international organizations, create new legal obligations, codify existing customary law, or develop progressive new norms. Once ratified, treaties become binding under the principle of pacta sunt servanda (agreements must be kept), though states may enter reservations to specific provisions or withdraw under certain circumstances defined by the treaty itself or general international law.

2. Customary International Law

Customary international law consists of unwritten rules and practices that have become so established, consistent, and accepted by nations over extended periods that they are considered legally binding, even without formal codification in treaties or written documents. This source of international law emerges from the actual behavior of states (state practice) combined with their belief that such behavior is legally required (opinio juris sive necessitatis).

For a practice to become customary international law, it must meet two essential criteria: First, there must be consistent and general state practice, meaning that a significant number of states engage in the behavior over a substantial period of time. Second, states must follow this practice out of a sense of legal obligation rather than mere courtesy, convenience, or political expediency. This subjective element distinguishes legal customs from mere habits or traditions.

Examples of customary international law include the prohibition on genocide, the principle of diplomatic immunity, the freedom of navigation on the high seas, and the prohibition on the use of force except in self-defense or with Security Council authorization. Customary law can bind even states that have not explicitly consented to it, though persistent objectors who consistently oppose a developing custom may not be bound. Customary international law is particularly important because it fills gaps where treaties do not exist and can evolve more quickly than formal treaty law to address new challenges.

The International Court of Justice frequently relies on customary international law when deciding cases, and many customary rules have been subsequently codified in treaties, creating an interplay between these two primary sources of international law. Determining the exact content of customary law can be challenging and often requires examining state practice through diplomatic correspondence, official statements, military manuals, domestic legislation, and the decisions of international and national courts.

3. General Principles of Law

General principles of law represent fundamental legal concepts and doctrines that are recognized across different legal systems and civilized nations worldwide. These principles serve as a supplementary source of international law, used primarily when treaties and customary law do not provide clear guidance on a specific issue. They help fill gaps in international law and ensure that the international legal system can address novel situations and disputes.

These principles are derived from comparative analysis of domestic legal systems, identifying common legal concepts that transcend particular national traditions and reflect universal notions of justice and fairness. They include both procedural principles (such as the right to a fair hearing, the principle that no one should be a judge in their own case, and the burden of proof) and substantive principles (such as good faith, equity, estoppel, and the prohibition on abuse of rights).

Key examples include: Good faith - the principle that parties must act honestly and fairly in their dealings; Equity - the concept of fairness and justice that allows courts to reach fair outcomes even when strict legal rules might produce unjust results; Res judicata - the principle that matters already decided by a competent court should not be relitigated; Unjust enrichment - the idea that one party should not benefit unfairly at another's expense; and Force majeure - circumstances beyond a party's control that excuse non-performance of obligations.

International courts and tribunals regularly invoke general principles when interpreting treaties, filling gaps in international law, and ensuring that the international legal system operates coherently and justly. These principles provide flexibility and adaptability to international law, allowing it to evolve and respond to new challenges while maintaining consistency with fundamental legal values shared across different legal traditions and cultures worldwide.

WHAT IS INTERNATIONAL LAW?

International Law: Foundation of Global Order

International law consists of a comprehensive system of rules, principles, norms, and standards that govern the relations and dealings of nations with each other, as well as the increasingly complex relations between states and individuals, relations between international organizations, and the interactions between states and non-state actors such as multinational corporations and non-governmental organizations.

This legal framework serves multiple critical functions in the international system: it provides predictability and stability in international relations, establishes mechanisms for peaceful dispute resolution, protects fundamental human rights, facilitates international cooperation on shared challenges, regulates the use of force and armed conflict, promotes economic development and trade, and protects the global environment. International law has evolved significantly from its origins in regulating diplomatic relations and warfare to encompass virtually every aspect of international interaction in our interconnected world.

The development of international law reflects humanity's ongoing effort to create order, justice, and cooperation in a world of sovereign states with competing interests. While it faces significant challenges in enforcement and compliance, international law remains essential for managing global interdependence and addressing transnational problems that no single state can solve alone.

Public International Law: Regulating State Relations

Public international law governs the relationships between sovereign nations and international entities, establishing the legal framework for how states interact with each other and with international organizations. This branch of international law is what most people think of when they hear the term "international law," and it encompasses the vast majority of international legal rules and institutions.

Public international law includes numerous specialized sub-fields: Treaty law governs the formation, interpretation, and termination of international agreements; Law of the sea regulates maritime zones, navigation rights, resource exploitation, and marine environmental protection; International criminal law defines and prosecutes genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity, and aggression; Laws of war or international humanitarian law establishes rules for armed conflict to protect civilians and limit suffering; International human rights law protects fundamental rights and freedoms of individuals against state violations; and Refugee law defines the rights of refugees and the obligations of states to provide asylum and protection.

Additional important areas include international environmental law, international trade law, diplomatic and consular law, the law of international organizations, and the law governing state responsibility for internationally wrongful acts. Public international law operates primarily through treaties, customary international law, and the decisions of international courts and tribunals such as the International Court of Justice. It reflects the principle of sovereign equality while recognizing that states must cooperate and accept certain limitations on their sovereignty to address common challenges and maintain international peace and security.

Private International Law: Resolving Cross-Border Disputes

Private international law, also known as conflict of laws, addresses legal disputes between private citizens, businesses, or other non-state entities from different countries. Unlike public international law which regulates relations between states, private international law determines which country's laws should apply to a particular dispute and which court has jurisdiction to hear the case when the dispute involves parties or events connected to multiple countries.

This field has become increasingly important in our globalized world where individuals and businesses routinely engage in cross-border transactions, marriages, employment relationships, and other activities that may give rise to legal disputes. Private international law addresses fundamental questions such as: Which country's courts can hear a divorce case when spouses are from different countries? Which country's law governs a contract between companies in different nations? How can a judgment from one country's court be enforced in another country? Which law applies when someone is injured in a foreign country?

The key principles of private international law include: Jurisdiction - determining which country's courts have the authority to hear a case; Choice of law - deciding which country's substantive law should apply to resolve the dispute; and Recognition and enforcement - determining whether and how judgments from foreign courts will be recognized and enforced domestically. These issues are typically governed by domestic legislation, international conventions (such as the Hague Conventions on private international law), and bilateral treaties between countries.

Private international law is essential for facilitating international commerce, protecting individual rights in cross-border situations, and ensuring that legal disputes involving international elements can be resolved fairly and efficiently. It provides the legal framework that enables individuals and businesses to engage confidently in international activities, knowing that legal mechanisms exist to resolve disputes and enforce rights across national boundaries.
